Did George Floyd’s murder and the subsequent protests affect attitudes toward local police budgets? Surveys conducted after Floyd’s murder may miss attitudinal change among urban residents, and past studies of police violence have not examined attitudes toward budgets. I examine two cities where millions in police spending is regularly subject to popular vote and where external events forced both cities to hold referendums just weeks after Floyd’s murder. Police spending is overwhelmingly popular in both cities before 2020, with majorities in nearly all precincts voting in favor; in 2020, average support drops and there is considerable heterogeneity across precincts. Areas with more Black residents and closer to local police killings see especially large decreases. In these two cities, the immediate effect of the Floyd protests was to modestly reduce support for police spending overall and to polarize support along racial lines.
